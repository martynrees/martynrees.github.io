Zigbee

Jul 11, 2022

Having had zigbee in the home for a couple of years, and getting more and more devices spread across the home, I figured it was time to understand a little more about the protocol, and also work toward my CWISA certification.

Zigbee is a wireless networking protocol that is primarily used for low-power, low-data rate applications in the Internet of Things (IoT). It is based on the IEEE 802.15.4 standard, which defines the physical and media access control (MAC) layers of the communication stack.

One of the key features of Zigbee is its low power consumption, which makes it well-suited for use in battery-powered devices. This is particularly useful in IoT applications, where devices may need to operate for long periods of time without access to a power source. Zigbee also has a relatively low cost and is easy to implement, which makes it a popular choice for IoT developers.

Another advantage of Zigbee is its mesh networking capabilities. In a mesh network, each device can act as a router, forwarding data to other devices in the network. This allows for increased reliability and coverage, as data can be transmitted over multiple hops if necessary. It also makes it easy to add new devices to the network, as they can simply join an existing network without the need for a central hub.

One of the main applications of Zigbee in the IoT is in home automation. Zigbee-based smart home systems are capable of controlling a wide range of devices, such as lights, thermostats, security systems, and appliances. These systems can be controlled remotely via a smartphone app or through voice commands to a virtual assistant, such as Amazon’s Alexa or Google’s Assistant, or my preference, Home Assistant! Building a robust Zigbee network across the home can be quick and easy, as long as you build out the network with powered Full Function Devices (FFD’s), as a network with only battery powered Reduced Function Devices (RFD’s) wont provice the mesh coverage needed, due to RFD’s not being able to act as co-ordinators or routers. Each zigbee network requires one co-ordinater, and can have many routers. It’s always ideal to place powered devices around key places in the home to ensure that mesh coverage is achieved when adding sensors such as temperature or presence sensors that are often only battery operated.
