80211 Roaming

Oct 10, 2022

Wireless roaming is an essential feature for any wireless network, especially for those that are deployed in large, multi-building environments such as universities, hospitals, and corporate campuses. With wireless roaming, users are able to move between different areas of the network without experiencing any disruption in their internet connection. This ensures a seamless and uninterrupted user experience.

One of the key technologies that enables wireless roaming is 802.11r, which is a standard that was developed by the Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE) to improve the speed and efficiency of wireless roaming. In this blog post, we’ll take a closer look at 802.11r and how it works to improve wireless roaming.

First, it’s important to understand how wireless roaming works in general. When a device connects to a wireless network, it associates itself with a particular access point (AP). As the device moves around, it may enter an area where the signal from its current AP becomes weak, while the signal from another AP becomes stronger. In this case, the device will perform a “handoff,” or a transition, to the new AP, in order to maintain a strong and stable connection to the network.

The handoff process can take some time, and during this time, the device may experience a temporary disruption in its internet connection. This is because the device must first disconnect from its current AP, then scan for available APs, select a new AP, and finally, authenticate and associate with the new AP.

This is where 802.11r comes in. This standard speeds up the handoff process by allowing the device to pre-authenticate with other APs while it is still connected to its current AP. This way, when it’s time to perform the handoff, the device can immediately transition to the new AP without having to go through the authentication process again. This helps to reduce the amount of time it takes for the handoff to complete, resulting in a smoother and more seamless wireless roaming experience for users.

In addition to improving the speed of the handoff process, 802.11r also enhances the security of wireless roaming. It does this by using a feature called “Fast Transition,” which allows the device to securely roam between APs without having to re-authenticate with the network’s authentication server. This helps to protect against potential attacks that could compromise the security of the network.

Overall, 802.11r is an important standard that helps to improve the speed and efficiency of wireless roaming. By reducing the time it takes for devices to transition between APs, 802.11r helps to ensure a seamless and uninterrupted user experience on wireless networks.
